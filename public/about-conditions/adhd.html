About ADHD

ADHD (Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder) is a brain condition that affects how people pay attention, sit still, and control their behavior. It's one of the most common mental health conditions in children and often continues into adulthood. People with ADHD have differences in brain development that make it harder to focus, organize tasks, and control impulses. It's not caused by bad parenting, too much screen time, or lack of willpower.

Common Symptoms

ADHD symptoms fall into two main groups. Inattention symptoms include trouble focusing on tasks, getting easily distracted, forgetting things, losing items, and having difficulty following instructions or finishing projects. Hyperactivity and impulsivity symptoms include fidgeting, trouble sitting still, talking too much, interrupting others, and acting without thinking about consequences. Some people have mostly attention problems, others have mostly hyperactivity, and many have both types of symptoms.

How It's Treated

ADHD treatment usually works best when it combines different approaches. Medications like stimulants (Adderall, Ritalin) or non-stimulants can help improve focus and reduce hyperactivity. Behavioral therapy teaches coping skills, organization strategies, and ways to manage symptoms. Lifestyle changes like regular exercise, good sleep habits, and structured routines also make a big difference. For children, working with schools to create supportive learning environments is very important.

What to Do Next

If you think you or someone you care about might have ADHD, talking with a mental health professional is the best first step. A proper evaluation can help determine if symptoms are from ADHD or something else. Remember that ADHD is very treatable - with the right support, people with ADHD can succeed in school, work, and relationships. Many successful people have ADHD and use their unique strengths to their advantage.
